# Databricks One

## 1. Executive Summary

Databricks One is a stripped-down, consumer-grade surface that gives business users governed access to AI/BI dashboards, Genie, and Apps without exposing clusters, notebooks, or the technical workspace.

## 2. Primary Purpose

- **Why it exists:** most employees who consume data are not engineers, yet the standard workspace assumes they are.
- **Business problem:** data products stay locked behind a technical UI, so adoption stalls at the analyst and engineer tier.
- **Technical problem:** removes clusters, notebooks, SQL editors, and jobs from view, leaving only the consumption surfaces a business user needs.
- **Governance angle:** every dashboard, Genie space, and app is still governed by Unity Catalog, so simplifying the UI does not weaken access control.

## 3. Where It Fits in the Lakehouse

```text
Business Users / Executives / Non-technical staff
        ↓
Databricks One  ◀ HERE  (consumer surface)
        ↓
Consumption Surfaces   (AI/BI Dashboards, Genie, Apps)
        ↓
Governance / Identity  (Unity Catalog, SSO, permissions)
        ↓
Data & Compute         (SQL Warehouses, Serverless)
        ↓
Storage                (Delta Lake)
```

- Sits at the **presentation edge** as a curated entry point, not a new engine or storage layer.
- Presents only **governed consumption artifacts** already built in the full workspace.
- Reuses the **same Unity Catalog permissions** — it narrows the UI, not the security model.
- A **routing and access layer**; the dashboards, Genie spaces, and apps live where they always did.

## 10. Architecture Alternatives

| Alternative | Type | Trade-off vs this feature |
|---|---|---|
| Full workspace access | In-Databricks | More capable but overwhelming and riskier for non-technical users. |
| AI/BI Dashboards direct links | In-Databricks | Shares individual assets; no unified, curated business-user home. |
| Genie shared directly | In-Databricks | Conversational only; not a single consumption surface. |
| Embedding dashboards in an external portal | External/In-Databricks | Fits existing portals; separate auth and no unified Databricks entry point. |
| Separate BI tool (Power BI, Tableau) | External | Familiar to business users; adds another tool, license, and governance seam. |
| Custom internal portal over APIs | External | Full UI control; you build and own auth, governance, and maintenance. |

## 11. Strengths

- Removes clusters, notebooks, and jobs from view — far less to misuse or misread.
- Same Unity Catalog governance as the full workspace; no parallel access model.
- Low training burden for business users and executives.
- Single curated entry point for dashboards, Genie, and apps.
- Natural-language access via Genie without any SQL.
- Broadens adoption beyond the analyst and engineer tier.
- No new engine to operate — reuses existing consumption artifacts.
- Reduces accidental exposure of compute and authoring tools.
- Serverless-backed queries need no cluster management by the consumer.
- Consistent, app-like experience that fits non-technical expectations.

## 12. Limitations

- Consumption only — no authoring of dashboards, Genie spaces, or apps.
- Relatively new; feature set and availability are still evolving.
- Not a fit for power analysts who need ad hoc SQL.
- No data engineering, pipeline, or ML surfaces.
- Value depends entirely on well-built upstream dashboards and Genie spaces.
- Curation and content management still happen in the full workspace.
- Not a replacement for external-portal embedding scenarios.
- Limited customization of the surface itself.
- Genie answer quality depends on upstream semantic modeling.
- Admin and governance configuration remain outside the surface.

## 13. Common Mistakes

- Expecting business users to author content here instead of just consuming it.
- Assuming it changes permissions — it inherits Unity Catalog, nothing more.
- Rolling it out before dashboards and Genie spaces are actually ready.
- Neglecting Genie semantic setup, then blaming poor answers on the surface.
- Using it where external-portal embedding was the real requirement.
- Treating it as a replacement for a full BI tool for heavy analysts.
- Forgetting that curation and upkeep still require full-workspace effort.
- Under-scoping UC grants and exposing more data than intended.

## 14. Design Recommendations

- ✔ Build and curate dashboards, Genie spaces, and apps in the full workspace first.
- ✔ Scope Unity Catalog grants to what each business audience should see.
- ✔ Invest in Genie semantic models so natural-language answers are trustworthy.
- ✔ Use it to broaden adoption, not to replace authoring or engineering surfaces.
- ✔ Keep power analysts on Databricks SQL; route consumers here.
- ✔ Choose dashboard embedding when the target is an existing external portal.
- ✔ Treat the surface as a curated home page — organize content deliberately.
- ✔ Confirm feature availability, since the surface is still maturing.

## 18. Cost Considerations

- **Licensing:** part of the platform; no separate authoring license for consumers. Verify current pricing.
- **Compute:** backing dashboards, Genie, and apps consume serverless DBUs when queried, billed as usual.
- **Storage:** none added — data stays in existing Delta tables and is billed there.
- **Operational Overhead:** low for the surface itself; effort is in curating and maintaining upstream content.
- **Training:** minimal for business users; the learning curve stays with the teams building dashboards and Genie spaces.

## 19. Executive Takeaway

- ✔ **Best for:** giving business users and executives a simple, governed home for dashboards, Genie, and apps without the technical workspace.
- ✔ **Avoid when:** users need to author content, run ad hoc SQL, or do data engineering and ML work.
- ✔ **Biggest benefit:** broadens adoption beyond technical users while keeping Unity Catalog governance intact.
- ✔ **Biggest limitation:** consumption-only and still maturing, so it depends on well-built upstream content.
- ✔ **Overall recommendation:** adopt as the standard business-user entry point on Databricks; keep authoring, engineering, and external-portal embedding on their existing paths.
