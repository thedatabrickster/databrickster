# Apps on Databricks Marketplace

> The application comes to your data instead of your data going to the application — partner software installs into your workspace, on serverless, governed by Unity Catalog, with nothing exported.

*Announced at Data + AI Summit 2026 (June 16–17, 2026). Source: [Databricks blog](https://www.databricks.com/blog/announcing-apps-databricks-marketplace).*

**Stage:** <StatusBadge stage="Public Preview" />

## What it is

- **A marketplace for installable apps, not just data sets.** Discover a third-party app in the catalog, authorize its permissions, and it deploys inside your own workspace — governed by Unity Catalog, with **no data movement**.
- **Install flow is genuinely one-click:** find it, install it, it auto-provisions on **serverless compute** in your secure workspace, you use it. No cluster to size, no infra to run.
- **Isolation model:** each app gets **per-app sandboxing** on **dedicated compute isolation**, outbound traffic is consumer-controlled through **Serverless Egress Gateway policies**, and providers ship **closed-source** so their IP stays protected.
- **Apps reach the real platform, not just tables** — Unity Catalog, SQL Warehouse, Lakebase, Model Serving, Foundation Model APIs, Genie spaces, and Agent Bricks are all addressable.
- **20 launch partners** at preview, including **Capital One Software, The Trade Desk, Datavant, Slalom, Prometheux, and Reflex**.

## Where it fits

- The **application / consumption layer** on top of the lakehouse — where a vendor ships software that runs where your data already lives, rather than pulling a copy into their SaaS.
- Teams that want vendor capabilities — data quality, observability, vertical analytics — **without exporting data** or standing up their own hosting for someone else's product.
- Pairs naturally with **Unity Catalog** for permission scoping and with **serverless** so there is no cluster to babysit for an app you did not write.
- Apps that need models or agents wire into **Model Serving, Foundation Model APIs, Genie spaces, and Agent Bricks** instead of reimplementing that stack — a real reason to buy in-platform.
- **Lakebase + SQL Warehouse** access lets apps do transactional and analytical work in place, which is what closes the gap with standalone products.
- Strong fit for **regulated shops** where "the data never leaves" is a hard procurement line, not a preference.

## Where it falls short

- **Catalog depth is the whole game, and it is thin.** 20 partners is a launch, not a category. Count the apps that actually beat your current tooling before you build anything around this.
- **The security review burden moves to you.** Sandboxing and an egress gateway lower the risk floor; they do not remove your obligation to vet each app's permissions, data access, and the vendor behind it.
- **Egress control trades directly against functionality.** Lock the gateway down and you break apps that legitimately need outbound calls; loosen it and the isolation story weakens. Tuning that policy is your job, per app.
- **Protected IP means opaque apps on governed data.** Closed-source software you cannot inspect, reading production tables, is exactly the auditability gap your security team will push back on — the IP protection that helps vendors hurts your review.
- **Snowflake Native Apps got here first.** This is a real category Snowflake has shipped for years. Evaluate maturity, partner overlap, and pricing head-to-head — do not grade it on the keynote.
- **Adopting it deepens lock-in.** Apps built to the Databricks runtime, Unity Catalog, Lakebase, and Agent Bricks are not portable; every one you install ties another workflow to the platform.

## My take

The architecture is right, and the framing — run software on governed data with no movement — is genuinely how enterprises want to buy now. Unity Catalog governance plus reach into Genie and Agent Bricks is a real differentiator over a plain "native app" runtime, and for a regulated shop the "data never leaves" property alone can justify a look.

But be honest about what this is: a **me-too** answer to Snowflake Native Apps, and the one thing that decides a marketplace's fate — how many apps people actually want to install — a 20-partner preview cannot yet prove. Everything else can be excellent and it still fails if the catalog stays shallow.

My recommendation is **watch the platform, pilot one app**. If a specific partner app solves a concrete need and keeps data in-platform, trial it in a low-sensitivity workspace and read every permission it requests before it touches anything real. Do not adopt a marketplace for its own sake, and judge it against Snowflake, not the launch slide. The unresolved question is not the plumbing — it is whether your security team will sign off on closed-source apps reading production data.

**Bottom line:** architecturally sound and strategically overdue — pilot a single real app on low-sensitivity data, and let the catalog prove itself before you commit.
