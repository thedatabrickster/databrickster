# Lakehouse//RT

> Databricks is finally going after the one workload the lakehouse always punted on: fast, concurrent, point-and-dashboard queries — without copying data into a separate serving store.

*Announced at Data + AI Summit 2026 (June 16–17, 2026). Source: [Databricks blog](https://www.databricks.com/blog/introducing-lakehousert-real-time-performance-unified-lakehouse).*

**Stage:** <StatusBadge stage="Beta" /> — select read-only workloads; 30% intro pricing through Jan 2027.

## What it is

- A real-time query engine — **Reyden** — that runs millisecond queries directly on Delta and Iceberg, governed by Unity Catalog, with **no data movement** to a separate serving tier.
- Databricks cites up to **16x** better performance than real-time serving layers, ~**10ms** on small data, and **sub-100ms at 12,000 queries/second** on standard analytical benchmarks — and, notably, that it handles **joins, window functions, and subqueries** that specialised engines often choke on.
- **AUTO sizing** (no t-shirt sizing) with incremental autoscaling that adds single nodes rather than spinning up whole warehouse copies.

## Where it fits

- The last mile of the lakehouse story: **operational analytics, app back-ends, and embedded dashboards** that need concurrency and low latency on governed data.
- Teams running a **separate serving tier today** — Postgres read replicas, ClickHouse/Druid/Pinot, or a warehouse serving layer — purely to hit latency SLAs. This collapses that copy and its second set of pipelines and permissions.
- Shops **already standardized on Unity Catalog + Delta/Iceberg**, who want one governance boundary across batch and real-time.

## Where it falls short

- It's **Beta and read-only** right now — that rules out most write-heavy operational patterns until it matures.
- The numbers are **Databricks' own, on "standard benchmarks."** Plausible for a purpose-built engine, but the interesting question is *your* query shapes and concurrency, not a keynote slide.
- The incumbents it targets — ClickHouse, Druid, Pinot, Rockset — are mature and genuinely fast. "Good enough on the lakehouse" has to hold up under real p99 and complex joins, not just averages.

## My take

This is the announcement I'd watch most closely as a platform lead. The recurring tax in every lakehouse shop is the **serving copy** — a second system, a second pipeline, a second permission model — that exists *only* because the lakehouse couldn't hit interactive latency. If Reyden removes that copy and keeps Unity Catalog governance intact, that's a real architectural simplification, not just a faster query.

But "Beta, read-only, vendor benchmarks" is exactly the stage where I **pilot, not commit**. I'd take one real dashboard or app-query pattern that currently lives on a separate engine, put it on Lakehouse//RT, and measure p99 under production concurrency with my actual joins. If it holds, this quietly retires a whole class of infrastructure.

**Bottom line:** the most strategically interesting thing at the Summit for lakehouse-committed teams — pilot it now against a real latency-bound workload, but don't decommission your serving tier until *your* p99 numbers say so.
