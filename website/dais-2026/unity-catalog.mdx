# Unity Catalog — Agentic-Era Governance

> Governance stopped being a data-only problem. Unity Catalog now treats models, agents, and MCP services as first-class governed objects — organized into three pillars: **Control, Context, Choice.**

*Announced at Data + AI Summit 2026 (June 16–17, 2026). Source: [Databricks blog](https://www.databricks.com/blog/whats-new-unity-catalog-data-ai-summit-2026).*

**Stage:** <StatusBadge stage="Mixed" /> — most updates are GA; a few are Beta / Preview (called out below).

## What shipped

- The whole thing is reframed as **governance for the agentic era**, sorted into three pillars: **Control, Context, Choice** — and governance scope now explicitly covers **models, agents, and MCP services**, not just tables.
- **Control:** Unity AI Gateway (GA), AI Gateway Budgets / spend caps (GA), Unified Agent Tracing (GA), ABAC (GA; ABAC Grant Policies for models in Beta), Contextual Service Policies (Beta), and RBAC (Public Preview soon).
- **Context:** Metrics (GA), external lineage (GA), column-level popularity (GA), Domains (Public Preview), and a business Glossary (Preview soon).
- **Choice / Openness:** four-level namespace (GA), managed disaster recovery (GA, needs the Mission Critical add-on), geospatial types (GA), a multimodal `FILE` type (Beta), and OpenSharing (now a Linux Foundation project).
- Databricks cites **14,000+ organizations** on Unity Catalog and **hundreds of thousands of agents** already acting on enterprise data.

## Where it fits

- This is the **governance and security layer** — the control plane sitting under agents, Genie, and the AI platform. If agents touch enterprise data, they touch this.
- Best fit for teams building **agentic workloads** who need one place to set what an agent may touch and to trace what it actually did.
- **AI Gateway Budgets** and the Unity AI Gateway land exactly where finance and platform teams lose sleep — runaway agent and model spend.
- **ABAC + Contextual Service Policies** are for orgs that have outgrown pure RBAC and need attribute- and context-driven access.
- **Metrics, Domains, Glossary** pair with Genie and BI so natural-language and agent queries resolve the same business terms the same way.
- **External lineage + column-level popularity** give governance teams impact analysis and a way to prune dead assets.

## Where it falls short

- **Breadth is complexity.** Three pillars spanning roughly 15 features is a large surface. Most orgs adopt a fraction and still owe the migration and training bill for the whole model.
- **Preview-vs-GA sprawl.** The core is genuinely GA (Gateway, Metrics, external lineage, geospatial, four-level namespace), but Contextual Service Policies, the `FILE` type, RBAC, and Glossary are Beta or Preview. Sequence adoption to real status, not to the keynote slide.
- **Four-level namespace migration is not a toggle.** Changing the namespace model ripples through code, grants, and integrations. Budget it as a project.
- **Governing autonomous agents is genuinely hard.** Policy and tracing help, but non-deterministic agents acting on live data leave audit, blast-radius, and accountability gaps that no governance UI fully closes. The honest test is whether spend caps and tracing constrain a *misbehaving* agent — not whether they render a clean lineage graph.
- **Open-catalog lock-in.** Deep Unity Catalog features cut against open catalogs — **Iceberg REST catalog**, **Apache Polaris / Snowflake Open Catalog**. The more governance logic you push in here, the harder it is to leave. Keep table formats interoperable even if you centralize policy.

## My take

The Control/Context/Choice framing is a clean story, and it's pointed the right way. Governance for tables was a solved problem; governance for hundreds of thousands of agents acting on live data is not, and that is where the risk is actually moving. Extending first-class governance to models, agents, and MCP is the correct bet.

**Adopt the GA core now** if you're already on Unity Catalog. Metrics, external lineage, ABAC, and the four-level namespace are low-risk, high-value, and mostly just work. **Pilot the agent-governance pieces** — Unity AI Gateway, Unified Agent Tracing, AI Gateway Budgets — against one real agentic workload before you standardize. They're largely GA but new, and their value lives entirely in whether the guardrails actually bite. **Watch the pre-GA edges** — Contextual Service Policies, `FILE` type, RBAC, Glossary — and confirm timelines before you build a dependency on them.

The one thing I'd hold firm on: the open-catalog camp (Iceberg REST, Polaris, Snowflake Horizon) is the real competitive check. Centralize *policy* in Unity Catalog if you like, but keep your *table formats* open so the catalog stays a choice.

**Bottom line:** Strong, coherent governance vision — adopt the mature GA core, pilot the agent-governance pieces, and keep your table formats open so the catalog stays a choice, not a cage.
