# AI-First Data Engineering

> An LLM call becomes just another SQL function in your ETL — which is exactly right ergonomically and exactly where the cost and eval problems start.

*Announced at Data + AI Summit 2026 (June 16–17, 2026). Source: [Databricks blog](https://www.databricks.com/blog/ai-first-approach-data-engineering-lakeflow-and-agent-bricks).*

**Stage:** <StatusBadge stage="GA" /> — AI functions are GA; **Genie for data engineering** is coming soon.

## What shipped

- LLMs called **inline from SQL** as ETL functions: `ai_extract`, `ai_classify`, `ai_translate`, `ai_parse_document`, and the general-purpose `ai_query` — orchestrated by **Lakeflow Jobs** on one governed platform.
- Covers entity extraction, classification, translation, document parsing, and arbitrary LLM transforms — all in plain SQL, no separate inference service.
- `ai_parse_document` runs multimodal models to pull text and tables out of PDFs, reason over figures, and caption images.
- **Serverless batch inference** (behind `ai_query`) auto-provisions and parallelizes compute, "reducing runtimes from hours to minutes" on high-volume workloads.
- Proof points: **Kard** categorizes across **billions of transactions** with these functions; **Banco Bradesco** cut coding time **50%** with Databricks Assistant; **Locala** orchestrated LLM training pipelines on **Lakeflow Jobs**.
- Coming soon: **Genie** for authoring and debugging pipelines in natural language.

## Where it fits

- **Unstructured-to-structured** ingestion: fields out of PDFs, emails, and free text into Delta tables via `ai_parse_document` / `ai_extract`.
- **Classification and tagging** at scale — transaction categorization, moderation, ticket routing — where a rule engine is too brittle to maintain.
- Lives in the **transform layer**: pairs cleanly with Lakeflow Declarative Pipelines and Unity Catalog for lineage and governance.
- Best for **batch**, latency-tolerant jobs — overnight enrichment, backfills, one-off corpus processing.
- Removes real friction for **SQL-first teams** who don't want to own a Python inference service.
- Strongest when outputs feed **human review or a downstream model**, not a hard automated decision.

## Where it falls short

- **Cost at scale is the headline risk.** One LLM call per row across billions of rows makes token spend, not CPU, the bill — and it can dwarf normal ETL compute.
- **Nondeterminism.** `ai_*` outputs drift run-to-run; reruns, backfills, and CDC replays may not reproduce, which breaks idempotency assumptions and complicates audits.
- **Eval is entirely on you.** SQL makes the call trivial and does nothing to measure accuracy, drift, or hallucination. No eval harness means no idea whether it's right.
- **Prompt governance gap.** Prompts buried in SQL have no obvious versioning, review, or A/B path — and verbose inputs (big documents) blow up token cost with no easy per-job cap.
- **Lock-in.** Pipelines wired to Databricks-managed endpoints are harder to port than a decoupled inference step.

## My take

**Pilot it.** The abstraction is the right one — making an LLM "just another SQL function" is where data engineering is heading, and Databricks executes the obvious move well. The ergonomics for unstructured extraction are genuinely good. The economics are the open question, so prove them on your own data before anything reaches production.

Convenience hides a metering problem: one careless `ai_query` over a big table is a four-figure surprise, and today the tooling for cost caps and eval isn't as first-class as the function itself. Run a bounded pilot with a **hard token budget** and a **labeled eval set**. Adopt now only for low-volume or high-value document parsing where per-row cost is negligible.

For billions of rows, benchmark inline SQL against a **dedicated batch-inference pipeline** (Ray, provisioned throughput, or a purpose-built service) — it may win on cost and control. And treat this as table stakes, not novelty: **Snowflake Cortex AISQL** and **BigQuery ML.GENERATE_TEXT / AI.GENERATE** do the same thing, so evaluate on price-per-token and eval tooling.

**Bottom line:** Great for unstructured extraction — treat anything row-by-row over huge tables as a cost experiment until your own numbers say otherwise.
