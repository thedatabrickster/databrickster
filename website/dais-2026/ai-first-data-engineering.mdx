# AI-First Data Engineering

> Call LLMs as SQL functions inside your ETL — extract, classify, translate, and parse documents without leaving the pipeline.

*Announced at Data + AI Summit 2026 (June 16–17, 2026). Source: [Databricks blog](https://www.databricks.com/blog/ai-first-approach-data-engineering-lakeflow-and-agent-bricks).*

**Stage:** <StatusBadge stage="GA" label="AI functions" /> <StatusBadge stage="Coming soon" label="Genie for DE" />

## Overview

- Embed AI **directly in ETL** via SQL functions — `ai_extract`, `ai_classify`, `ai_translate`, `ai_parse_document`, and the general `ai_query` — orchestrated by **Lakeflow Jobs** on one governed platform.
- Covers entity extraction, classification, translation, document parsing, and general LLM transforms — all expressed in **plain SQL**.
- `ai_parse_document` uses multimodal foundation models to parse text, extract tables, reason over figures, and turn images into AI-generated descriptions.
- **Serverless batch inference** (which powers `ai_query`) automatically provisions and scales compute and runs workloads in parallel, "reducing runtimes from hours to minutes" for high-volume AI workloads.
- Customer proof points: **Kard** uses Agent Bricks AI functions for scalable, accurate transaction categorization across **billions of transactions**; **Banco Bradesco** cut coding time by **50%** with Databricks Assistant; **Locala** used **Lakeflow Jobs** to orchestrate complex LLM training pipelines earlier schedulers couldn't handle.
- Coming soon: **Databricks Genie** for pipeline authoring and debugging via natural language.

## Where it fits

- **Unstructured-to-structured** ingestion: pull fields out of PDFs, emails, and free text into Delta tables with `ai_parse_document` / `ai_extract`.
- **Classification/tagging** at scale — transaction categorization, content moderation, ticket routing — where a rule engine is too brittle.
- Sits in the **transform layer**: pairs naturally with Lakeflow Declarative Pipelines and Unity Catalog for lineage and governance.
- Best for **batch**, high-volume, latency-tolerant jobs — overnight enrichment, backfills, one-off corpus processing.
- Lowers the barrier for **SQL-first data engineers** who don't want to stand up a separate Python inference service.
- Good fit when outputs feed a **human-in-the-loop** review or a downstream model, not a hard automated decision.

## Where it fails

- **Cost at scale is the headline risk.** An LLM call per row across billions of rows (see Kard) can dwarf normal ETL compute — token spend, not CPU, becomes the bill.
- **Nondeterminism.** `ai_*` outputs vary run-to-run; reruns, backfills, and CDC replays may not reproduce, complicating idempotency and audits.
- **Quality and eval are on you.** SQL makes the call trivial; it does nothing to measure accuracy, drift, or hallucination. No eval harness = no idea if it's right.
- **Prompt governance gap.** Prompts embedded in SQL are config-as-code with no obvious versioning, review, or A/B story.
- **Token-cost blowups** from verbose inputs (large documents in `ai_parse_document`) are easy to trigger and hard to cap per-job.
- **Lock-in**: pipelines wired around Databricks-managed model endpoints are harder to port than a decoupled inference step.

## Response

- **Verdict: Pilot.** The ergonomics are genuinely good for unstructured extraction; the economics are the open question — prove them on your data first.
- Run a **bounded pilot with a hard token budget** and a labeled eval set before any production rollout.
- **Adopt now** only for low-volume or high-value document parsing where per-row cost is negligible.
- For **billions of rows**, benchmark against a **dedicated/external batch-inference pipeline** (Ray, provisioned throughput, or a purpose-built service) — inline SQL may lose on cost/control.
- Market context: this competes conceptually with **Snowflake Cortex AISQL** and **BigQuery ML.GENERATE_TEXT / AI.GENERATE** — the SQL-LLM pattern is now table stakes, so evaluate on price-per-token and eval tooling, not novelty.

## Personal impression

- The abstraction is right: making an LLM "just another SQL function" is where data engineering is heading, and Databricks is executing the obvious move well.
- But convenience hides a metering problem — one careless `ai_query` over a big table is a four-figure surprise. The tooling for **cost caps and eval** needs to be as first-class as the function itself.
- **Hype level: Medium.** Real and useful, but the "hours to minutes" gain is workload-specific and the cost story is unproven at the scales being advertised.
- **Bottom line:** Great for unstructured extraction; treat anything row-by-row over huge tables as a cost experiment until your own numbers say otherwise.
