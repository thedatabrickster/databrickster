# OpenSharing & SecureConnect

> Delta Sharing grows up: share models, agents, and governed Genie Agents across clouds and vendors — and a Databricks-managed proxy finally kills the per-recipient firewall dance that stalls most external sharing deals.

*Announced at Data + AI Summit 2026 (June 16–17, 2026). Sources: [Databricks blog — OpenSharing](https://www.databricks.com/blog/introducing-opensharing-next-evolution-delta-sharing-agentic-era) · [Databricks blog — SecureConnect](https://www.databricks.com/blog/introducing-opensharing-secureconnect).*

**Stage:** <StatusBadge stage="Public Preview" /> — SecureConnect is Public Preview; the OpenSharing spec is open (Linux Foundation).

## What shipped
- OpenSharing extends Delta Sharing beyond tables: share data (Delta, Iceberg, Parquet), AI models, autonomous agents, and **Genie Agents** across any cloud, vendor, or on-prem, all governed by Unity Catalog.
- No copies — recipients query live data at the source, with end-to-end governance and audit logging in Unity Catalog.
- The protocol now lives at the **Linux Foundation** (opensharing.io): an open spec plus Databricks' enterprise implementation.
- **Iceberg REST Catalog API** support means any Iceberg client can read shares; providers can also share from external catalogs (AWS Glue, Hive Metastore, Snowflake Horizon).
- On-prem storage partners: **MinIO (GA)**, **Everpure (Private Preview)**, with Qumulo and VAST Data next, and Cohesity, Commvault, NetApp, Nutanix by year-end.
- **SecureConnect (Public Preview)** — a Databricks-managed proxy that routes storage access for recipients: providers allowlist Databricks endpoints once instead of per-recipient IPs. Zero config for serverless recipients, optional NCC private link, mutual TLS, and data never leaves the provider's bucket.
- Scale today: **28,000+ recipients**, and **33% of shares are cross-platform**.

## Where it fits
- The outbound edge of the lakehouse — the collaboration layer for partners, subsidiaries, and customers.
- Teams sharing across multiple clouds or vendors who currently hand-maintain firewall allowlists for every single recipient.
- Productizing analytics: ship a governed Genie Agent to a customer instead of raw tables.
- Model and agent distribution — platform teams handing curated models and agents to downstream business units.
- Pairs with **Unity Catalog** as the single governance plane and with **serverless recipients** that get the zero-config SecureConnect path.
- Fits hybrid and on-prem shops via the MinIO/VAST/Qumulo partner story plus Iceberg REST interop.

## Where it falls short
- **Cross-vendor agent sharing has no governance or liability story.** Who owns a bad answer, whose audit logs, whose prompt policy wins when a Genie Agent runs on someone else's platform? Largely unaddressed — and that is the whole flashy part.
- **The managed proxy is a new dependency and trust boundary.** SecureConnect becomes a single control point in the path of every external share — convenient until it is the thing that is down.
- **"Open" sits in tension with a Databricks-managed control plane.** The spec is Linux Foundation, but SecureConnect is Databricks-operated infrastructure recipients lean on. The portable part and the useful part are not the same part.
- **Zero-config is serverless-only.** Everyone else still touches NCC and private link, so "configure once" is partial.
- **Preview and partner maturity risk.** SecureConnect is Public Preview, most on-prem partners are Private Preview or "coming soon", and cross-vendor agent sharing is new enough that RBAC, audit, and revocation semantics will have early gaps. Egress economics of live cross-cloud reads go unquantified — model your own.

## My take
SecureConnect is the quietly useful piece, and it is the reason to pay attention. Per-recipient firewall coordination is a boring, real problem that kills external sharing deals in security review, not in engineering. Removing it — allowlist Databricks once, data stays in the provider's bucket, mutual TLS — is a concrete win with low blast radius. **Pilot it** alongside data and model sharing, but validate failure modes and support posture first: it is Public Preview, and it is about to sit in the path of your production shares.

Cross-vendor Genie Agent sharing is the flashy bet and the riskiest. Shipping a governed conversational agent to a customer instead of raw tables is a genuinely good idea; doing it across vendors before liability, audit, and revocation are spelled out is a lawyer's field day. **Watch this one** — do not expose agents externally until those answers exist. Competitively this is up against Snowflake Data Sharing and cloud-native shares (AWS Data Exchange, BigQuery Analytics Hub); the differentiator is agent/Genie sharing plus managed networking, not raw data sharing, which everyone already does. Lean on the Linux Foundation spec as your portable fallback so you are not fully bound to the proxy.

**Bottom line:** pilot the managed networking and data/model sharing now; treat cross-vendor agent sharing as a Watch until governance and liability are in writing.
