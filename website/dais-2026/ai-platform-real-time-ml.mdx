# AI Platform — Real-Time ML

> Raw data to a production endpoint without leaving Unity Catalog: an ML-engineering agent, serverless GPUs by the click, and serving that survives real QPS.

*Announced at Data + AI Summit 2026 (June 16–17, 2026). Source: [Databricks blog](https://www.databricks.com/blog/whats-new-ai-platform-agents-ml-engineering-our-deep-learning-platform-and-new-capabilities).*

**Stage:** <StatusBadge stage="GA" /> — Model Serving & Genie Code for ML are GA; **AI Runtime** is in Public Preview.

## What shipped

- Three pieces: **Genie Code for ML Engineering** (**GA**), the **serverless AI Runtime** deep-learning platform (**Public Preview**), and enhanced **real-time Model Serving** (**GA**).
- **Genie Code for ML** is Unity Catalog-aware and wires natively into Feature Store, Serverless Compute, AI Runtime, Model Serving, and Inference Tables — feature engineering, training/tuning, and deployment to endpoints, with MLflow across the lifecycle.
- **AI Runtime** attaches **serverless A10 and H100 GPUs in 2–3 clicks**, **pay-per-use** (no idle time, no upfront commit), plus **multinode training**, **RDMA**, and optimized data loading; orchestrated via Lakeflow Jobs and DABs.
- **High-QPS Model Serving** claims **300K+ QPS at under 10ms p99 latency overhead**, **100K+ QPS in production**, CPU and GPU models, **up to 2x** better p99/p50, and infra costs cut **up to 90%+** versus self-managed.
- **Feature Store** adds declarative features (define once, materialize for train and serve), **Streaming Features**, and online serving on **Lakebase**.
- **Genie ZeroOps for ML** brings agentic observability — inference-table querying, endpoint debugging, and alert root-cause analysis.

## Where it fits

- Classic-ML teams already on Databricks who want feature store, training, and serving inside **one Unity Catalog governance boundary** instead of three stitched systems.
- Genie Code targets the **glue work** — feature pipelines, training scaffolds, deploy configs, monitoring hooks — which is exactly where MLOps teams bleed hours.
- Serverless A10/H100 suits **bursty training and fine-tuning** without standing up and babysitting a GPU cluster.
- Real-time serving fits **fraud, recommendations, pricing, and personalization** — fresh features, user-facing latency, sustained load.
- Pairs with **Lakeflow** (ingest/orchestrate), **Lakebase** (online features), and **Genie ZeroOps** (root-cause layer).
- Strongest if you already run MLflow and feature engineering here and want to kill a separate serving stack.

## Where it falls short

- **GPU access is the real gate.** "2–3 clicks to an H100" is meaningless if capacity is queued in your region — validate actual availability and pay-per-use rates before you plan around it.
- **The headline QPS is overhead, not a promise.** 300K+ QPS at under 10ms p99 is *platform latency overhead*; your end-to-end p99 rides on model size, payload, and feature-fetch cost.
- **"Up to" hides the typical case.** Up to 2x latency and up to 90%+ cost are best-case ceilings — treat them as marketing bounds, not planning numbers.
- **This is classic-ML territory, not GenAI.** Feature stores and real-time endpoints are classic-ML concerns; confirm the specific pieces you need aren't GenAI-first afterthoughts.
- **Mature incumbents already own this.** SageMaker (feature store, real-time endpoints, multinode training) and Vertex AI (feature store, online serving) are GA and battle-tested — Databricks is the challenger here.
- **AI Runtime is Public Preview.** Expect rough edges, and don't bet a production training pipeline on it before GA. UC-aware convenience is also **lock-in** — it only pays off if features, models, and serving all stay in Databricks.

## My take

Strategically this is the right bet. Unifying feature engineering, training, serving, and monitoring under Unity Catalog — with an agent doing the glue — is precisely where MLOps teams lose the most time, and it's the natural extension of a lakehouse into ML. The serving numbers are now concrete (300K+ QPS, under 10ms p99 overhead, up to 2x latency) rather than hand-wavy, which is a real step up.

But this is the most crowded, most mature category Databricks competes in. SageMaker and Vertex have done real-time serving and feature stores for years, and nobody rips out a working endpoint stack for a challenger. The wedge here is governance, not raw capability — one UC model across the whole lifecycle.

**Verdict: Pilot.** If you already run ML on Databricks and feel the feature-store-to-serving pain, scope one real-time workload and benchmark **your** end-to-end p99 under production QPS, feature freshness, and cost per million predictions — not the platform's overhead number. Adopt Genie Code cautiously on low-risk workflows first; a coding agent spanning feature-eng through monitoring is a wide surface. If you're happy on SageMaker/Vertex, there's no forced-migration case.

**Bottom line:** A credible, now-GA serving and agent story for Databricks-committed ML shops — pilot one workload against your own p99 and cost, and don't reflexively migrate off a working SageMaker/Vertex stack.
