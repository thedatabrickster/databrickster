# Genie Ontology

> Genie's answers are only as good as its context. Ontology tries to build that context automatically — mining meaning from your tables, queries, and dashboards instead of asking you to hand-curate a semantic layer.

*Announced at Data + AI Summit 2026 (June 16–17, 2026). Source: [Databricks blog](https://www.databricks.com/blog/introducing-genie-one-genie-ontology-and-genie-agents).*

**Stage:** <StatusBadge stage="Not stated" /> — the announcement did not give a release stage.

## What it is

- An **automatic context layer** that harvests knowledge from **tables, queries, dashboards, pipelines, and connected apps** and assembles it into a **living graph** — no hand-built semantic model required.
- Captures **metric definitions, business terms, and unique calculations**, plus the **relationships** between concepts, metrics, tables, and teams.
- Ranks trustworthiness with a **PageRank-like heuristic** — weighing source origin, author authority, reliance frequency, connection to certified assets, and freshness.
- **Feeds both Genie One and Genie Agents**, giving them business context and a signal for which sources to trust.
- Enforces permissions through **source-native ACLs or Unity Catalog** — it inherits governance rather than reinventing it.

## Where it fits

- The **context layer** of the Genie stack — the shared meaning that both **Genie One** (the AI coworker) and **Genie Agents** (domain agents) draw from.
- Best for orgs where **the same metric means different things** across dashboards and everyone wants one authoritative definition.
- Speeds up **onboarding agents or analysts** who lack the tribal knowledge of how metrics, tables, and teams actually relate.
- Pays off anywhere **trust ranking** matters — surfacing the certified, frequently relied-on source over the stale or ad-hoc one.
- Sits over governed data, respecting existing **Unity Catalog** and source-native permissions, so it fits a locked-down estate without a new access model.

## Where it falls short

- **Auto-extracted meaning can be wrong.** A graph learned from usage inherits whatever bad patterns live in your queries and dashboards, and it drifts as sources change. Auditability of learned context is the open question.
- **The ranking is a heuristic, not truth.** A PageRank-style score rewards what's popular and well-connected — not necessarily what's correct. A widely reused wrong definition can outrank the right one, and the scoring itself is opaque.
- **It overlaps with Metric Views.** Conceptually it competes with **Unity Catalog Metric Views**, **dbt Semantic Layer**, **LookML**, and **Cube**. Teams already invested there face a dual-maintenance decision, not a clean swap.
- **Lock-in.** A Databricks-native context graph keeps your definitions inside the platform; portability to non-Databricks BI or agents is unproven.
- **Curation doesn't disappear.** "Automatic" cuts setup, but authoritative definitions and certified assets still need human stewardship to stay meaningful over time.

## My take

The problem is real and worth solving. Inconsistent numbers across tools is genuine enterprise pain, and traditional semantic layers die from curation burden — nobody keeps them current. Building context automatically and ranking it by trust is a genuinely smart attack on that failure mode. This is the right direction.

The catch is that it trades one problem for another. Auto-extraction plus a popularity-style ranking introduces a drift-and-trust problem: the wrong definition can be the well-connected one, and you won't always see why the graph picked it. Until I can watch its ranking behave on a messy domain, I wouldn't make this the system of record for what a metric means.

So: **pilot it** on one domain where definitions already conflict, and verify the ranking surfaces the sources you'd actually certify. **Don't rip out dbt/LookML/Cube/Metric Views** — run in parallel and compare governance, drift control, and portability. The semantic-layer war is active; Databricks' edge here isn't the concept, it's the native Genie and agent integration.

**Bottom line:** on-strategy and worth a pilot on your messiest domain — just don't let an auto-built graph become your source of truth for definitions until it proves it can hold meaning steady.
