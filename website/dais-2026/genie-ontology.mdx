# Genie Ontology

> An automatic context layer that extracts business knowledge from across your platform and organizes it into a living graph — so Genie answers grounded in how your company actually works.

*Announced at Data + AI Summit 2026 (June 16–17, 2026). Source: [Databricks blog](https://www.databricks.com/blog/introducing-genie-one-genie-ontology-and-genie-agents).*

## Overview

- An **automatic context layer** that extracts snippets of knowledge from **tables, queries, dashboards, pipelines, and connected apps**, and organizes them into a **living graph**.
- Captures **metric definitions, business terms, unique calculations**, and the **relationships** between concepts, metrics, tables, and teams.
- Ranks trustworthiness with a **PageRank-like approach** — weighing source origin, author authority, reliance frequency, connection to certified assets, and freshness.
- **Powers both Genie One and Genie Agents**, giving them full business context and the ability to identify trustworthy sources.
- Enforces permissions through **source-native ACLs or Unity Catalog**.

## Where it fits

- The **context layer** of the Genie stack — feeds both **Genie One** (the AI coworker) and **Genie Agents** (domain-specific agents) shared business meaning.
- Best for orgs where **the same metric means different things** across dashboards and teams want one authoritative, trusted definition.
- Helps when **onboarding agents or analysts** who lack tribal knowledge of how metrics, tables, and teams relate.
- Useful anywhere **trust ranking** matters — surfacing the certified, frequently relied-on source over stale or ad-hoc ones.
- Sits over governed data, respecting existing **Unity Catalog** and source-native permissions.

## Where it fails

- **Auto-extraction cuts both ways.** A graph built automatically from usage can absorb bad patterns or drift as sources change — trust and auditability of learned context is the open question.
- **Ranking is a heuristic, not truth.** A PageRank-style score favors what's popular and connected, which is not always what's correct; a widely reused wrong definition can win.
- **Overlap with existing semantic tooling.** It competes conceptually with **Unity Catalog Metric Views**, **dbt Semantic Layer**, **LookML**, and **Cube** — teams already invested there face a dual-maintenance decision.
- **Lock-in risk.** A Databricks-native context graph keeps definitions inside the platform; portability to non-Databricks BI or agents is unproven.
- **Curation still matters.** "Automatic" reduces setup, but authoritative definitions and certified assets still need human stewardship to stay meaningful.

## Response

- **Pilot** — the shared-context problem is real; test it against one domain where metric definitions already conflict.
- **Watch** how the ranking behaves before making the graph your system of record for definitions — validate that certified sources actually win.
- **Don't rip out dbt/LookML/Cube/Metric Views** yet — run in parallel and compare governance, drift control, and portability.
- **Market context:** the semantic-layer war is active — dbt, Looker/LookML, and Cube all claim the "one definition" high ground; Databricks' edge is native Genie + agent integration, not the concept itself.

## Personal impression

- The pitch is right — inconsistent numbers across tools is genuine enterprise pain, and grounding Genie in shared business meaning is the correct direction.
- The **living graph plus trust ranking** is a smart twist: it attacks the curation-burden problem that kills traditional semantic layers by learning context automatically.
- But automatic extraction plus a popularity-style ranking adds a **drift-and-trust** problem — the wrong definition can be the well-connected one.
- **Hype level: Medium.** **Bottom line:** promising and on-strategy — pilot it on one messy domain, verify that its ranking surfaces the definitions you'd certify, and keep your existing semantic tooling until it proves it can hold meaning steady over time.
