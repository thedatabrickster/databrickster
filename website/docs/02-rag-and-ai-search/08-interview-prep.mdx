## Section A — The idea of RAG

**A1. In one sentence, what problem does RAG solve?**

<details>

It lets a model answer using *your* private or current data — by finding relevant passages and putting them in the prompt — instead of guessing from what it memorized during training.

</details>

**A2. What do the three letters in RAG stand for, and what happens at each step?**

<details>

Retrieve (find the most relevant passages from your data), Augment (add them into the prompt), Generate (the model writes an answer grounded in that text).

</details>

**A3. Why does RAG reduce hallucinations?**

<details>

Because the model reads real, supplied facts instead of relying on memory. And because you can attach citations, a human can verify each claim.

</details>

**A4. Why is RAG usually preferred over retraining the model on your documents?**

<details>

No retraining needed: your data stays in your own store, updates take effect instantly when documents change, it's far cheaper, and access control stays with your data.

</details>

## Section B — Preparing and indexing data

**B1. Why don't we embed a whole 50-page document as one vector?**

<details>

Two reasons: it won't fit the embedding model's token limit, and its meaning becomes a blurry average of many topics that matches no specific question well. So we split it into focused chunks.

</details>

**B2. What is chunk overlap and why use it?**

<details>

It's a bit of repeated text shared between neighboring chunks (often 10–20%). It's a safety margin so an idea that lands on a boundary isn't cut in half — it survives whole on at least one side.

</details>

**B3. What's the difference between a Vector Search endpoint and an index?**

<details>

The endpoint is the compute that hosts indexes and answers queries. The index is the searchable catalog built from one Delta table. One endpoint can host many indexes.

</details>

**B4. What is a Delta Sync index, and why do data engineers love it?**

<details>

An index that embeds a text column for you and stays in sync as the Delta table changes — like a materialized view for AI search. Your table stays the source of truth; the index follows it automatically. (It needs Change Data Feed enabled on the table.)

</details>

## Section C — Retrieving and answering

**C1. Why must the query use the same embedding model as the index?**

<details>

Different models produce vectors in different "coordinate systems," so a query vector from one model can't be meaningfully compared to chunk vectors from another. Using the index's `query_text` path avoids this automatically.

</details>

**C2. What are metadata filters, and why are they critical for security?**

<details>

They restrict which chunks are eligible before ranking (like a SQL `WHERE`). For security, a filter scoped to the authenticated user (e.g. `client_id`) guarantees the search can never return another user's documents — even a perfect semantic match. Source the filter value from the session, never from user input.

</details>

**C3. What are the two rules that make a RAG answer trustworthy?**

<details>

(1) Ground only in the retrieved text. (2) Say "I don't know" when the context doesn't contain the answer. You enforce both in the system prompt, and back up rule 2 with a code guardrail when retrieval returns nothing.

</details>

**C4. A RAG answer is wrong. What's the very first thing you check — and what do you NOT do?**

<details>

First: look at the *retrieved chunks* and ask whether the needed info is even in them (recall). Do NOT immediately rewrite the prompt — if it's a retrieval (wrong-book) problem, coaching the model to "summarize better" won't help.

</details>

**C5. Contrast hybrid search and reranking.**

<details>

Hybrid search blends keyword (exact-term) and semantic (meaning) scoring so exact codes/acronyms and paraphrases both match — it improves the *candidates*. Reranking is a second, careful pass that re-scores the top candidates by reading each one together with the question and keeps the best few — it improves the *ordering*. "Retrieve wide → rerank → answer narrow."

</details>

## Section D — Cost & storage

**D1. Name the four cost centers of a RAG system, and which is paid per question.**

<details>

Embedding the corpus (once, then on refreshes), storage, the Vector Search endpoint (compute while it's up), and the generation call. The **query embedding** and the **generation call** are paid **per question**; generation is usually the largest per-query cost.

</details>

**D2. Costs jumped but traffic was flat. Where do you look first?**

<details>

Index-time costs — most likely a **full re-embed** of the whole corpus (e.g. a nightly rebuild) instead of incremental re-embedding of only changed chunks. Also check for a `CONTINUOUS` or idle endpoint left running.

</details>

**D3. How does `top_k` show up on the bill?**

<details>

Every retrieved chunk becomes input tokens in the generation prompt, billed per token on **every** query. Higher `k` = bigger prompt = more cost and latency (and sometimes worse answers). Retrieve fewer, better chunks (rerank) to keep it down.

</details>

**D4. Estimate storage for 3 million 1024-dim vectors, and say what else adds to it.**

<details>

1024 × 4 bytes ≈ 4 KB each → ~12 GB for vectors alone. Budget ~2–3× more for the source text, metadata, index overhead, and retained Delta history (until you `VACUUM`).

</details>
