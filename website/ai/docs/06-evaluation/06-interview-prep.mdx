## Section 1 · Why evaluating AI is hard

You spent years trusting `assert result == expected`. Let's see why that trusty friend struggles here.

**1. Why can't we usually check a GenAI answer with a simple exact-match comparison?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

Because there is rarely a single correct answer. A good response can be phrased in many different ways, all of them valid. Exact-match would fail a perfectly good answer just because the wording differs from what we wrote down. GenAI outputs are open-ended, so "did it match this exact string" is the wrong question.

</details>

**2. If we can't compare strings, how do we actually decide whether an answer is good?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

We grade it against a rubric, the same way a teacher grades an essay. Instead of one pass/fail check, we score the answer on several dimensions, such as correctness, groundedness, relevance, and safety. Each dimension asks a focused question about the response, and together they give a fuller picture of quality.

</details>

**3. What do we mean by dimensions like "correctness," "groundedness," "relevance," and "safety"?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

They are separate qualities we care about:

- Correctness: is the answer actually right?
- Groundedness (also called faithfulness): is the answer supported by the source material we gave it, rather than made up?
- Relevance: does it actually address what the user asked?
- Safety: is it free of harmful, toxic, or inappropriate content?

Grading each one separately tells us not just that something is wrong, but in what way.

</details>

**4. What is the difference between offline evaluation and online monitoring?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

Offline evaluation runs against a fixed, curated dataset before you ship. Think of it as a gate: you check quality on known examples and decide whether the change is good enough to deploy.

Online monitoring watches real, live traffic after you ship. It is ongoing and never really "finishes." It tells you how the system behaves with actual users, which is always messier than your test set.

</details>

**5. Why do we want both, instead of just picking one?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

Offline eval catches regressions early and lets you compare versions fairly on the same examples, but it can only test cases you thought of. Online monitoring catches the surprises real users bring, but by then the code is already live. Together they cover both "before you ship" and "after you ship."

</details>

## Section 2 · Datasets, scorers, and judges

Now the building blocks: what you evaluate against, and what does the grading.

**6. What is in an evaluation dataset?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

At minimum, a set of questions or inputs. Often each example also carries an optional expected answer, or a list of key facts the answer should contain. The expected answer is not required for every kind of scoring, but it helps a lot when you have it.

</details>

**7. Where do the examples in an evaluation dataset come from?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

Three common sources:

- Real traces: actual questions users have asked your system.
- Experts: people who know the domain writing good question-and-answer pairs.
- Synthetic: examples generated by a model, then reviewed by a human before they are trusted.

The "then reviewed" part matters for synthetic data. You never blindly trust generated examples.

</details>

**8. Why should evaluation datasets be versioned, and how do they grow over time?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

Versioning means you can compare results fairly. If the dataset changed between two runs, a score change might just be the dataset, not the model. Freezing versions keeps comparisons honest.

They grow by adding real failures. Every time production surprises you with a bad answer, that case becomes a new example in the dataset, so you never regress on the same mistake twice.

</details>

**9. What is the difference between a heuristic scorer and an LLM judge?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

A heuristic scorer uses a fixed rule or measurement, no AI involved. Examples: exact-match, latency, cost, whether a keyword appears. They are cheap, fast, and perfectly consistent.

An LLM judge is a model asked to grade another model's output against a rubric. It handles the fuzzy, open-ended judgments that a simple rule cannot, like "is this answer relevant and well-supported."

</details>

**10. Which built-in dimensions might a judge score, and roughly what does each check?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

Common built-in ones include:

- Correctness: is it right?
- Relevance: does it answer the question?
- Groundedness / faithfulness: is it supported by the retrieved context, not invented?
- Safety: is it free of harmful content?
- Retrieval quality: did the system fetch the right supporting documents in the first place?

</details>

**11. How would you run an evaluation over a dataset in code?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

You point `mlflow.genai.evaluate` at your dataset and hand it a list of scorers. It runs your app on each example, applies each scorer, and collects the results so you can compare runs. You can mix built-in scorers with your own.

</details>

**12. When would you write a custom scorer or custom judge?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

When your quality bar is specific to your use case and the built-ins don't capture it. Maybe you need to check that an answer always cites a policy number, or follows a particular tone, or never recommends a competitor. A custom scorer encodes your own rule; a custom judge encodes your own rubric.

</details>

## Section 3 · Human feedback and production monitoring

The last piece is the loop that keeps the whole thing honest.

**13. Judges are convenient, so why can't we fully trust them on their own?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

Because judges are imperfect. A model grading a model can be biased, inconsistent, or simply wrong. So we calibrate the judge against human labels: we check whether the judge agrees with trusted human judgments, and adjust until it does. An uncalibrated judge is just an opinion you haven't checked.

</details>

**14. What are some ways we collect human feedback on AI outputs?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

A few common ones:

- A Review App where reviewers look at traces and rate them.
- Labeling traces directly, attaching a judgment to real recorded interactions.
- End-user signals like thumbs up and thumbs down on a response.

Each turns a human's opinion into data you can use.

</details>

**15. How does human feedback form a loop that improves everything else?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

Human labels do double duty. First, good examples become new entries in your evaluation dataset, so future tests reflect real-world cases. Second, those same labels calibrate your judges, so automated grading stays aligned with what humans actually think. Feedback flows back in, and the whole system gets a little more trustworthy each time.

</details>

**16. What does production monitoring actually do, and what is it watching for?**

<details>
<summary>Show answer</summary>

It runs scorers on a sample of live traces and tracks quality, latency, and cost over time. You sample rather than score everything because grading all traffic would be expensive. Watching these trends over time lets you catch drift (quality slowly sliding) and regressions (a sudden drop after a change) before users complain.

</details>
